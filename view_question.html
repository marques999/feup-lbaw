I am asked this question a lot. People are disillusioned and frustrated with the “safe” advice from financial professionals, family, and friends to “Save and invest throughout your lifetime.”

Licensed Financial Service providers are required by law to provide this answer to the public because the public is not a “qualified investor.” According to the Securities Act of 1933 and defined in Regulation D, accredited/qualified investors are classified as meeting certain criteria, such as having an annual income of at least $200,000 in each of the past two years ($300,000 for joint income) or a net worth of at least $1 million. I know because I was a licensed financial services provider. I was required to give up my licenses in order to share with, and teach people the needed mindset, knowledge, and skills that I now share some of with you.

Once you accept that your life is a business and decide to manage your life as if you would a business, your next step is to build your coaching and mentoring team. This is critical to achieving your life goals, as well as achieving real security and freedom. For example, housing is an essential element to personal security. Yet most individuals and families are dependent upon their income to secure their housing. This course of action takes up 30-40% of income in either rent or mortgage payments. Perceived savings built in equity from a mortgage comes at the cost of interest paid over the lifetime of the mortgage. In addition, whether renting or owning property taxes must also be paid annually. Yes, renters pay the property tax on the home they are renting. However, it is an unrealized expense as the owner has it calculated and included in the monthly rents.

What most young 20 something do not know is that they can buy a 4-plex just as easy as a SFR (Single Family Residence). The difference is that if done correctly the rent from 3 of the 4 units will pay all expenses, i.e. mortgage, insurance, property taxes, maintenance, etc.. In addition, it will also add a small profit to monthly cash flow and allow you to keep the 30-40% of your earned income that would be going to pay the rent or mortgage in your pocket. The difference this makes may not seem significant, however it becomes painfully apparent during a recession/depression when people are losing their jobs, their source of income. Individuals who’s housing is dependent upon their earned income are among the first to lose their housing if they don’t find employment quickly. Whereas individuals who’s housing is based on their rental income, are a lot more secure having the security of rental income to pay the housing bills and keep food on the table.
